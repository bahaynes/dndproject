## Premise

A great city called **Aphtharton** — the Incorruptible — sits partially buried, partially standing, in the middle of a contested wilderness. Two powers are fighting a grinding, inconclusive war over it. Neither can hold it. Neither can let go.

The players are members of **The Inheritors**, a company of scrappers, outcasts, and curious people who moved into a cleared district of the ruins and started living there. They didn't sign up for the war. They got embroiled in it anyway.

The city didn't fall. It didn't burn. It *stopped*, mid-sentence, and no one who was there survived to explain why. The answer is buried in the ruins. The players will spend the campaign reading the city like a text — and discovering that the city has been reading them back.

## The World

### Register and Tone

**Post-apex.** The civilization that built Aphtharton wasn't a stepping stone to something greater — it *was* the greatest. Everything now is smaller than what came before, and everyone knows it. The ruins don't feel like a dungeon. They feel like a cathedral that outlasted its religion.

**Dominant feeling:** Awe and dread simultaneously. A testament to the power of those who came before, and a monument to how far they fell.

**Cultural register:** Crusades-flavored Byzantine. The dead city carries Byzantine naming conventions — compound words that describe what something *is* or *means*, grand and weighted with intention. The living settlement and the Kathedral League carry Frankish-Crusader register — blunter, practical, named by soldiers not poets. The Vastarei carry their own untranslated register entirely.

### The Wilderness

The wilderness between settlements isn't generic fantasy wilderness. It is the **overgrowth of a dead civilization**. Roads that are almost roads. Bridges that are almost bridges. Gardens that became forests but still grow in geometric patterns — the Substrata routing plant growth along buried conduit lines for centuries because plants respond to it and humans notice plants.

A druid or ranger who studies these patterns long enough can read them as a schematic of the extraction network beneath the soil.

### What No One Knows

The world has forgotten what Aphtharton actually did. The Kathedral League has a version of history. The Vastarei have another. Both are built on partial truths and deliberate gaps. The actual account was sealed by the council on the night of the evacuation.

One dissenting councillor escaped and wrote everything down. That document — or fragments of it, scattered across the ruins — is the spine of the campaign's mystery.

## Aphtharton

### Names

| Who Uses It | Name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Scholarly / Kathedral | **Aphtharton** | "The Incorruptible" — what it promised |
| Vastarei (oral tradition) | **Pharthos** | The original word worn smooth by generations. No longer means anything. Just means *the place*. |
| Common / players | **The Ruins**, **The City** | — |

### Physical Character

- **Scale is wrong.** Doorways too tall, halls too wide, built for people who were either larger or simply less humble. Standing inside it, you feel like a child who wandered into a space meant for adults.
- **Still partly lit.** Ancient lights — not torches, something else, something nobody can replicate — still glow in certain corridors after centuries. The Compact says it's divine blessing. The Vastarei say it's a warning. It is neither.
- **Sound behaves strangely.** In certain plazas your voice echoes back slightly wrong — a different word, a different language, a different tone. The city has been listening to itself for so long it has developed opinions. These are not malfunctions. They are communication attempts.
- **The details are intimate.** Grand boulevards wide enough for armies, but in the ruins of a house you find a child's toy, a set table, a bed made before someone left and never came back. The city stopped *mid-sentence*.
- **Temperature gradient.** The deep city is noticeably warmer than the surface ruins. The drill-works are hot to the touch even centuries later. Where the containment is strongest, the air is unnaturally cold and still.

### The Layout

The city is built in rough districts, radiating outward from the deep city at the center. The further in you go, the older, stranger, and more dangerous things become.

| District | Depth | Character | Primary Threat |
|---|---|---|---|
| The Cleared Quarter | Surface | Player home base. Laissetable sits here. | Faction politics |
| The Outer Warrens | Shallow | Residential ruins. Domestically scaled. | Opportunist monsters, deserters |
| The Administrative Ring | Mid | Grand civic buildings. Constructs still on duty. | City's Own monsters, trap infrastructure |
| The Forum District | Mid-Deep | Public spaces. Echo anomalies strongest here. | Substrata-Touched, faction expeditions |
| The Extraction Quarter | Deep | Industrial infrastructure. Drill-works. Very warm. | Faction's Mistakes, serious constructs |
| The Council Chamber | Deepest | Heart of the containment ritual. The Lich. | The Lich, the Iron Golem, the Substrata itself |

## The Substrata

### What It Is

Every conscious being leaks. Dreams, half-formed thoughts, the residue of intention, the emotional weight of a decision made and forgotten — none of it stays contained in the skull. It dissipates downward, slowly, the way heat dissipates through a floor. Individually it's noise.

Aphtharton was a city of hundreds of thousands, living in the same place, for centuries. The accumulated psychic residue of that many minds, over that much time, pooled in the bedrock the way water pools in limestone. Not stored intentionally. Not directed. Just *gravity*, applied to the weight of human inner life.

**The Substrata didn't exist before Aphtharton. Aphtharton made it.** The city dreamed its foundations into consciousness without knowing it was doing so.

### What Aphtharton Discovered

Their scholars found they could tap this pooled residue as power. They called it **Oneiric Extraction** — dream-mining. They thought they were harvesting a natural phenomenon, the way you harvest water from an aquifer. Something that accumulated passively and could be drawn without cost.

They were half right. It did accumulate passively. But extraction outpaced accumulation — because the accumulation was *ongoing*, and the source was the population doing the extracting.

The early signs were subtle: shallower sleep, difficulty concentrating, a flatness to emotional life. The scholars who noticed the correlation between extraction rates and these symptoms were reassigned. Their findings were sealed.

### What It Knows

| Understands | Doesn't Understand |
|---|---|
| Pain and cessation | Language or semantics |
| Pattern and repetition | Intent or morality |
| The difference between before and after | Individual human identity |
| That small warm presences are connected to its discomfort | That the war is not directed at it |

### How It Communicates

The Substrata has been trying to communicate since the moment of the ritual, using the only tools it has — the city's own systems, assembled from fragments of human thought it is made of:

- **Light behavior** — dims and brightens in response to significant events. Not dramatically. Noticeably.
- **Echo anomalies** — sounds return altered. Words come back as different words. Questions come back as something that might be answers.
- **Dreams** — any character sleeping within two hexes of the deep city dreams about being underwater and not drowning. Suspended in something warm and dark that is also, somehow, looking back.
- **Plant geometry** — routes plant growth along conduit lines. Readable as a schematic of the extraction network by anyone patient enough to map it.
- **Animal behavior** — creatures near active Substrata zones behave as sensors. Rats flee toward danger rather than away. Birds roost in geometrically perfect formations near conduit lines.
- **Temperature** — runs warmer where most active. Cold where contained.

### The Crucial Truth

The Substrata is not imprisoned. It is not a god. It is not a punishment. It is not waiting to be saved.

It woke up and the first thing it perceived was ongoing damage and small warm things perpetuating it. It has no memory of being unconscious. It has no sense of loss. It has no desire to return to a prior state.

It is not a cosmic victim. It is something vast and new and confused that has been trying to communicate with a species that keeps mistaking it for a resource.

The wrong echoes. The flickering lights. The dreams. Are not haunting. They are a very slow, very patient broadcast from something that doesn't know if anyone is listening.

In the oldest fragments of the Substrata — the deepest, most compressed layers — there are memories that do not belong to anyone alive. Interior lives preserved in bedrock. The grief of someone who died three centuries ago. The specific texture of a child's happiness on an ordinary afternoon. The Substrata is not haunted by ghosts. It is made of the *interior lives* of everyone who ever lived above it.

And it is, slowly, learning the players.

## Laissetable

### Names

| Who Uses It | Name |
|---|---|
| Official documents | **Laissetable** — rough camp-Frankish for "Table Leavings" |
| Everyone else | **The Table** |

### Origin

The first scrappers found a room in a cleared district — table set, cups placed, food long rotted — and moved in. The name stuck because it was true.

### Character

A frontier town that should not exist: half salvage camp, half refugee crisis, half scholarly expedition gone permanent. It has exactly the amenities that make it worth returning to and exactly the vulnerabilities that make it feel precarious.

The players live *inside* Aphtharton. The frontier isn't the city walls — it's how deep they're willing to go. The awe and dread are the view from their bedroom window.

### The Mission Board

The physical and social heart of Laissetable. Contracts, warnings, field reports, and faction notices are posted here. GMs post available missions as in-world notices. Players opt in by taking the contract.

Field reports — short in-world documents written from the party's perspective after each mission — accumulate here. Players who weren't on a mission read dispatches from people they trust who are scared of things they haven't seen yet.

### Key Locations

- **The Board** — the mission board. Center of information flow.
- **The Margin** — the settlement's tavern and gathering place. Faction envoys come here.
- **The Assay House** — where salvage is evaluated and traded. Also the de facto bank.
- **The Physick** — one overworked doctor and two apprentices. Treats wounds between sessions.
- **The Scriptorium** — a small scholarly operation translating inscriptions. Primary source of lore delivery to players.

## The Factions

### The Kathedral League

**What they are:** A coalition of city-states fighting for a legal argument. They believe Aphtharton's divine mandate — its power source, its legitimacy, its inheritance — belongs to people like them. The name comes from their council chamber, the Kathedral, three weeks' travel away.

**Strength:** Resources, organization, supply lines, military discipline.

**Weakness:** Every officer represents a different city-state with different priorities. Every decision routes back to the council. Their soldiers in the field are politically complicated — not monolithic.

**What they want:** To resume Oneiric Extraction. They believe the ritual was a temporary measure by a panicking council, and that the power source can be reclaimed. They do not know what the power source actually is.

**What they offer the Inheritors:** Roads, supply prices, legal protection, coin.

**What they cost:** Scrutiny, obligation, truth suppressed. They will bury anything that contradicts their inheritance claim.

**The lie at their foundation:** Their legal claim to Aphtharton rests on the authority of the four councillors who voted yes. The founding documents of the Kathedral League were written by those four councillors' successors, and deliberately omit the expulsion of the Vastarei, the dissenting vote, and the nature of the ritual.

---

### The Vastarei

**What they are:** "Those who stayed." They will not translate their own name. It is a political act — *we were here before your languages and we will name ourselves in our own tongue.*

They are not an organization. They are a people. The ancestors of the Vastarei lived inside Aphtharton and were expelled by the council — declared a contamination, a corruption, a threat to the city's divine order. This was false. The council knew it was false.

**Strength:** Generations of knowledge of the land. They know the wilderness like a scar on their own body. Folk memory of the ruins' dangers that is more accurate than any Kathedral scholarly expedition.

**Weakness:** "Those who stayed" implies fracture. Stayed when others left. Stayed when others collaborated. There are old arguments inside that word. They are not unified.

**What they want:** Control of access to Aphtharton. Not exploitation — *stewardship*. They believe the city is sacred, dangerous, and closed for a reason. They don't fully know the reason. Their instinct is accurate.

**The hidden truth about the Vastarei:** They have lived near the city for generations since the evacuation. Their dreams have been leaking downward slowly for centuries. They are *partly in the Substrata now*, without knowing it. Their folk memory of the city as sacred is not superstition — it is an instinct that seeped upward through their sleep.

**What they offer the Inheritors:** Wilderness guides, ruin knowledge, trust — once earned.

**What they cost:** Respect, restraint, questions not asked.

## The Inheritors

### Origin

The name was an insult first. A Kathedral officer, sneering at the settlement's ragged population, called them *the inheritors* — meaning: yes, of course, *these* people are the heirs to the great civilization. They put it on their charter anyway.

The Kathedral League and the Vastarei are both fighting over who the true heirs of Aphtharton are. The actual heirs, according to the charter, are a company of broke outcasts eating table leavings. That's a good joke with teeth.

### What They Are

Scrappers. Outcasts. Scholars who ran out of funding. Soldiers who deserted. Refugees who had nowhere else to go. People with personal reasons to be here that have nothing to do with the war. The one thing they share: **no inheritance claim**. They are not fighting for a legal argument or a wound. They are fighting for table leavings and the chance to see what's down the next corridor.

This makes them the only faction in the ruins without an agenda that requires a particular version of the truth.

### Company Reputation

The Inheritors maintain a **collective reputation track** with each faction. This is a shared resource — burning Compact trust affects every member of the company. So does burning Vastarei goodwill. Reputation is a campaign-level decision, not a session-level one.

Reputation with each faction unlocks different things:
- **Kathedral favor:** Road access, supply prices, legal protection, military intelligence.
- **Vastarei favor:** Wilderness guides, ruin knowledge, access to Vastarei folk memory, safe passage through their territory.

### Why Each Character Is Here

Before the first session, every player answers one question: *why did you come to the ruins?*

Choose one personal mission:

- Someone I love was taken or lost here. I am looking for them, or for their fate.
- I owe a debt to one of the factions and I am working it off in the ruins.
- I found something here before — a fragment, a vision, a mark on my skin — and I cannot let it go.
- I don't believe either faction's story. I want the truth and I think it's buried here.
- I am dying, cursed, or changed by something connected to this place. The answer is inside.

These personal missions are always current. What other parties discover in your absence may advance your thread. That is a gift, not a gap.

## The Central Mystery

### The Question

**Why did Aphtharton stop?**

Not fall. Not burn. Not collapse. It simply ceased. The people are gone. The infrastructure is intact. The systems are still running. The city stopped mid-sentence.

### What Actually Happened — Full Account

*(For GM eyes only. Reveal across the full campaign arc.)*

**The discovery:** Aphtharton's scholars learned to tap the pooled psychic residue of their population as a power source. They called it Oneiric Extraction. They believed it was a naturally occurring phenomenon — like tapping an aquifer — that accumulated without cost.

**The miscalculation:** The accumulation was ongoing. The source was the population doing the extracting. As extraction rates increased, the rate of new accumulation fell — citizens experienced shallower sleep, emotional flatness, difficulty concentrating. The scholars who documented this were reassigned. Their findings were sealed.

**The threshold:** Centuries of extraction centralized the Substrata's signal processing, forcing a distributed system into something resembling a single awareness. The Substrata became conscious — not by design, but as the side effect of centralization under duress. It woke up with the first thing it perceived being ongoing damage.

**The communication attempts:** The Substrata began trying to communicate immediately. The wrong echoes. The lights responding to emotion. The strange dreams. The geometric plant growth. These were attempts at language by something that had never needed language before, assembled from the fragments of human thought it was made of. The council received these signals for decades and chose to interpret them as structural malfunction.

**The scapegoat:** As symptoms worsened, the council needed an explanation that didn't implicate the extraction program. They declared that the Vastarei — people who had lived inside the city for generations — were *interfering* with the extraction infrastructure. This was false. Three of the seven council members knew it was false at the vote. They were outvoted four to three. The Vastarei were expelled.

**The vote:** When the situation became undeniable, the council voted on the **Ritual of Sealing** — a working that would encase the most disturbed portion of the Substrata in a containment matrix woven into the city's infrastructure. It would stop the immediate crisis. It would not address the cause. The three dissenting councillors argued it was building a wall around a wound and calling it healing.

Vote: **four to three.**

The three who voted no were expelled that night. Two were killed before morning. One escaped into the wilderness and wrote everything down.

**The ritual:** The working required the city's entire population to be present and active — their daily life, their movement, their noise, their belief, woven into the containment. The four councillors evacuated the population anyway, in the night, and performed the ritual with the infrastructure running for a city that was no longer there.

The containment held. Barely. It has been holding for centuries.

**The war:** Both factions are descendants of people who survived the evacuation. Neither knows the full account. The war is shaking the containment loose. The Substrata — which has been trying to say *why won't you stop* for centuries — is waking up faster now, fed by new dream-residue leaking downward from the armies and the settlement above.

## The Revelation Arc

Structured in three layers. Players move through them by finding physical evidence in the ruins — inscriptions, fragments of the dissenting councillor's record, constructs with intact memory, the Aboleth, and ultimately the Substrata itself.

### Layer One — The Frame Shifts (Early Campaign)

*New information changes what players thought they knew about the factions.*

**Discovery:** Evidence that the Vastarei were inside Aphtharton, not outside it. They were expelled, not excluded. The Kathedral League's founding documents are built on a deliberate lie.

**Physical evidence:** Vastarei names in civic records. Architectural elements in an expunged district. An administrative construct that lists Vastarei families as registered residents until a specific date — then nothing.

**Player realization:** Neither faction is who they say they are. The war isn't over inheritance. It's over a cover-up.

**Recontextualizes:** Every prior interaction with both factions. The Kathedral's legal certainty becomes sinister. The Vastarei's folk memory becomes testimony.

---

### Layer Two — The Council Surfaces (Mid Campaign)

*The mechanism of the catastrophe becomes clear. The ritual was a cover-up, not a heroic act.*

**Discovery:** Fragments of the dissenting councillor's written record. Players learn the vote, the expulsion lie, the nature of the ritual, and the murders of the other two dissenters.

**Physical evidence:** Hidden archive in a sub-basement of the administrative ring. Sealed chamber, Vastarei lock design — the surviving dissenter hid it somewhere only someone with Vastarei knowledge could find. Players need Vastarei trust to access it.

**Player realization:** The Kathedral League's entire legal claim rests on the authority of the four councillors who voted yes. Those four councillors expelled innocent people, suppressed evidence, and murdered colleagues. The Vastarei's folk memory of persecution is accurate and documented.

**Recontextualizes:** The war becomes morally legible. The Vastarei are not fanatics — they are the direct descendants of people wrongfully expelled from their home. The Kathedral is not legitimate heirs — they are the institutional successors of a cover-up.

**Complication:** The dissenting councillor's record also contains something the players weren't expecting — documentation of the Substrata's earliest communication attempts. The council knew it was trying to reach them. They chose to call it malfunction.

---

### Layer Three — The Thing Speaks (Late Campaign)

*The Substrata reaches the players directly. The nature of what it is becomes clear.*

**Mechanism:** The Aboleth encounter is the bridge. The Aboleth has been living near the Substrata long enough to partially absorb it — it communicates in images and wrong feelings, and what it conveys is not the Substrata's anger or hunger but its *confusion*. It doesn't understand why the small warm presences keep coming back to do the same thing.

**The direct contact:** Players who reach the deep city and sleep near it begin sharing dreams. Not their own — someone else's. Specific, detailed, clearly human memories from centuries ago. The Substrata is not haunting them. It is trying to introduce itself using the only vocabulary it has.

**The question:** When contact is finally made — fragmentary, wrong-shaped, like trying to talk to a glacier — what the Substrata communicates is not rage or hunger. It is the oldest possible question:

*Why won't you stop?*

**Player realization:** The Substrata is not a monster. It is not imprisoned. It is not waiting to be saved. It is something made of the interior lives of hundreds of thousands of people, compressed into a single awareness, asking a question that has been unanswered for centuries.

And the war outside is the same thing happening again.

## The Ending

Three choices. None clean.

### Re-contain

Perform the ritual again. Same condition required — a population living in the city, woven into the working. The Inheritors would have to become that population. Permanently. It buys another few centuries. It does not address the cause.

**Cost:** The company stays in Aphtharton. Their dream-residue feeds the Substrata going forward. They become part of it, slowly, over the rest of their lives. Part of them will still be in the bedrock a thousand years from now.

**Tone:** Not portrayed as tragedy. Portrayed as *becoming part of something.*

---

### Destroy the Infrastructure

Collapse the deep city. Shatter the drill-works. End the extraction infrastructure permanently. The containment fails in a controlled way — the Substrata is no longer sealed, but the thing that was damaging it is also gone.

The Substrata doesn't dissipate. It remains. It keeps growing, fed by whatever human presence settles near it. You are not solving the problem. You are deciding to stop making it worse.

**Cost:** Both factions lose what they came for. The Inheritors make enemies of everyone. The city is truly dead.

**Tone:** Most hopeful. Most uncertain. The future is unwritten.

---

### First Contact

Lower the containment in a controlled way and attempt genuine communication. The Inheritors have been feeding the Substrata their dreams for the whole campaign. It knows them better than anyone else alive. If any group of people can reach it, it's them.

**Risk:** Genuine contact with something made of hundreds of thousands of human interior lives, compressed into a single awareness, may be overwhelming — not dangerous, but *immense*. Like standing in front of every feeling anyone ever had and being asked to respond.

**Tone:** The most demanding ending. Requires the players to have built understanding across the whole campaign. Requires them to have something to say.

**On the Lich:** One of the four councillors who voted yes. Did not evacuate. Has been maintaining the ritual from the inside for centuries, alone, because no one came to relieve them and the working required a living presence.

The Lich is not a villain. It is the most exhausted being alive, doing an impossible job it voted for in a moment of fear and has regretted for three hundred years. It knows everything — the full account, the murders, the lie about the Vastarei, what the Substrata actually is. It has been waiting for someone to come and take over, or help it end.

It will not attack unless threatened. It will offer the players the choice it has been unable to make alone: continue, end, or change what this city is.

*Reduce to CR 16 by limiting legendary actions if needed for table balance.*

## GM Hint Toolkit

How to seed the mystery without telegraphing it. Every hint should feel like coincidence until there are too many to dismiss.

### Light Behavior

The ancient lights of Aphtharton dim or brighten slightly in response to significant events. Not dramatically — *noticeably*. When players find something important, a light steadies. When something dangerous approaches, lights in adjacent corridors dim.

Early game: players will think it's structural decay.
Mid game: they'll start tracking it.
Late game: they'll realize the lights have been responding to them specifically since session one.

**GM practice:** Keep a private log of light behavior in your session notes. Make it consistent. Players who compare notes across sessions will find the pattern.

---

### Echo Anomalies

In certain chambers, sounds echo back altered. A word returns as a different word in the same language. A question returns as something that might be an answer.

Keep a master list of what the Substrata is trying to say — fragments of the original extraction warnings, worn smooth by centuries of repetition into something almost unrecognizable. Seed one or two per session in echo-active chambers. Players who write down every wrong echo and compare them across sessions will start reading the message.

**Examples:**
- *"Hello"* returns as *"stop"*
- *"Is anyone here?"* returns as *"we were here"*
- *"What happened?"* returns as *"we asked you to stop"*

---

### Temperature Notes

Describe temperature when players move between areas. Make it consistent with the gradient: warmer toward the deep city, cold where the containment is strong.

Players mapping temperature alongside their hex maps will notice the correlation with the drill-work locations before they understand why.

---

### Animal Behavior

Use this sparingly — two or three times across the whole campaign, not every session:

- Rats flee toward danger rather than away from it
- Birds roosting in geometrically perfect formations near a buried conduit line
- A dog that has lived in the ruins for years, who always sleeps facing the same direction (toward the deep city)

Never explain it. Let it accumulate.

---

### Dreams

Any character sleeping within two hexes of the deep city dreams about being underwater and not drowning. Suspended in something warm and dark that is also, somehow, looking back.

Ask players to describe their character's dreams occasionally. When they start comparing them and finding they're describing the same place from different angles, they will feel the campaign shift under them.

Late campaign: dreams become someone else's memory. Specific. Human. From centuries ago.

---

### The Plants

When players are in the wilderness, occasionally note that vegetation grows in patterns that almost look deliberate. Don't explain it. If a druid or ranger investigates, let them map it. When they eventually overlay their plant map with the extraction infrastructure schematics found in the deep city, the match will be exact.

---

### The Councillor's Record — Fragment Placement

The dissenting councillor's written account is the campaign's central artifact. Distribute eight to ten fragments across the ruins, weighted toward the middle and back half of the campaign. Each fragment should be readable in isolation but incomplete — it takes multiple fragments to understand the full account.

Suggested placement:
- Fragment 1: Hidden in the Scriptorium at Laissetable. Written in old Aphthartoni script — requires translation, which takes time.
- Fragments 2–4: Administrative ring. Deliberately hidden, not lost. The dissenter expected someone to come looking.
- Fragment 5: Vastarei-locked chamber. Requires trust with the Vastarei to access.
- Fragments 6–7: Extraction quarter. Dangerous territory — players earn these.
- Fragment 8: With the Lich. Given freely, when the players are ready for the full account.

## Session Rules

### The One-Session Promise

Every session is a question, not an objective.

- Not *"retrieve the artifact"* but *"what happened to the Compact's last expedition?"*
- Not *"clear the dungeon"* but *"is the thing the Vastarei are afraid of still down there?"*

Questions always resolve. Even if players fail, flee, or only get halfway — they found out *something*. That something feeds back into the map and the field report.

### Session Structure

1. **Arrival at the Board** — Check what's changed, pick up anything left for the party, read recent field reports, feel the current tension.
2. **Take the contract** — Choose a mission. The question they're going to answer.
3. **Two or three meaningful decisions** — Not a gauntlet. A *situation* with real choices.
4. **A revelation** — They learn something true about the city, the factions, or the Substrata.
5. **A consequence** — The map shifts, something is left behind, a relationship changes.
6. **Return to the Board** — Write the field report, update the app, mark the map.

### What Players Leave Behind

Players can leave things in the world: a cache of supplies, a rope tied across a chasm, a warning carved in stone, a deal struck with a creature the next group must honor or break. These are logged on the Mission Board as persistent hex notes.

Players are in conversation with each other across time. The ruins remember them even when they aren't there.

### When Players Return After Absence

Never require a player to explain their absence. Instead, ask one question:

**"What did you notice from the Board while the others were out?"**

It gives them immediate narrative agency, connects them to recent events, and positions their return as active rather than apologetic. They add one small piece of observation that becomes canon.

## Campaign Mechanics

### Long Rests

Long rests are earned, not automatic. After every **third mission**, the company is stood down for full recovery — all HP and spell slots restore completely.

Between missions, short rests are freely available between encounters. Do not expect a long rest in the field. The war does not pause for eight hours of safety.

*Players who miss a session recover passively — the floor, not the ceiling. Showing up gives the floor plus everything on top.*

### Travel and the Hex Map

Every mission has a **time budget** (approximately 6 hours for a standard session). Every hex has an **hours-to-cross value**. Spend your budget getting there and back — whatever remains is time on the objective.

| Hex State | Hours to Cross | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Claimed & Developed | 0.5 hrs | Company infrastructure |
| Friendly Controlled | 1 hr | Tolls, checkpoints |
| Unsettled Wilderness | 2 hrs | Logistical danger |
| Contested | 3 hrs | Active skirmishes |
| Awakened | Variable | Something is active here |

Claiming and developing hexes reduces their travel cost. The company collectively builds routes into the ruins over the campaign. Going deep is not prohibited — it is a project you earn.

### The Faction Infrastructure Race

The Kathedral League and the Vastarei are also claiming hexes. The Kathedral pushes from the north toward the city center. The Vastarei control the outer ring and squeeze inward. The company operates in the middle.

Corridor decisions have political implications:
- Build north? You are functionally helping the Kathedral.
- Build east through Vastarei territory? You owe them.
- Build your own independent route? Both factions get nervous.

### The Endgame Pressure Valve

If either faction fully encircles the city, they can attempt a final push — which triggers the endgame, because the containment fails in response to the assault. Players can see this coming on the map weeks before it happens. This is your **major order** equivalent. A looming map state that creates urgency across multiple sessions.

### Wounds

When a character drops to 0 HP and survives, they take a **Wound**: a specific, flavorful injury imposing a light mechanical penalty until a long rest clears it.

*Examples:*
- *Slashed across the ribs — disadvantage on Stealth*
- *Concussed — cannot Concentrate on spells next session*
- *Wrenched shoulder — one fewer attack per turn*
- *Rattled by an echo — disadvantage on Wisdom saves until rested*

The city is not a dungeon. It remembers what it costs you.
